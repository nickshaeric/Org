#+title: Security Threat Categories
#+filetags: :lecture:
* Lecture Notes
Understanding how threats are categorized is foundational to designing effective defense strategies.
Threats differ based on origin, intent, and impact, and [[id:0be6b1f3-fb8c-4cde-b580-f7028e347518][cybersecurity]] practitioners use these distinctions to determine which controls to prioritize.

The four primary threat categories - External, Internal, Environmental, and Advanced Persistent Threats - map directly onto the actors, motivations, and tactics covered in [[id:41aad614-a6f6-4be2-b524-f2bc6897c85a][Threat Actors]], but approach the problem from an organizational rather than an actor-centric perspective.

A critical insight is that these categories are not mutually exclusive - a nation-state APT may begin as an external threat, establish an insider foothold, and persist for months, blending all four categories simultaneously.
All four categories ultimately threaten one or more pillars of the [[id:326ef9ea-d7ec-4034-967f-3465f91e8aa4][CIA Triad]], and understanding the category of a threat helps determine which defensive layer to prioritize.

* Key Concepts
** External Threats
*External threats* originate outside the organization's security perimeter and are carried out by hackers, cybercriminal organizations, nation-state actors, hacktivist groups, or competitors.
Motivations include financial gain, political disruption, intellectual property theft, and espionage.
Because external threats are the most visible category, they tend to drive the most investment in perimeter defense systems such as firewalls, IDS/IPS, and VPNs.
Key defense tactics: [[id:][Firewalls]], IDS/IPS, VPNs, and threat intelligence feeds.

** Internal Threats
*Internal threats* originate from within the organization - from current or former employees, contractors, third-party vendors, or interns who already have legitimate access to internal systems.
Internal threats are either *intentional* (sabotage, espionage, data theft) or *unintentional* (human errors, accidental misconfiguration, falling for phishing emails).
They are among the most dangerous threat categories precisely because insiders bypass perimeter defenses entirely - the threat originates from within the trusted boundary.
Key defense tactics: Role-Based Access Control (RBAC), least privilege principle, employee monitoring, exit procedures, and security awareness training.
Key Quote: "Internal threats are often more damaging because they already have access."
This connects directly to [[id:41aad614-a6f6-4be2-b524-f2bc6897c85a][Threat Actors]] and the insider threat discussion there, and to [[id:2df80c85-7f5a-4829-bf7c-a5bc82be2a4c][privacy]] concerns around over-collection of data.

** Environmental Threats
*Environmental threats* are non-human threats arising from natural or infrastructural factors - earthquakes, floods, fires, power outages, and hardware failure.
Their impact is primarily on *Availability* in the [[id:326ef9ea-d7ec-4034-967f-3465f91e8aa4][CIA Triad]], though they can also cause data corruption that violates Integrity.
Key defense tactics: Disaster recovery plans, off-site backups, redundant power supplies (UPS), and geographically distributed data centers.

** Advanced Persistent Threats (APTs)
*APTs* are highly sophisticated, stealthy, and prolonged cyberattacks orchestrated by well-funded, trained groups - typically nation-state entities or state-sponsored cyber units such as APT29 (Cozy Bear) and APT10.
Their lifecycle follows a consistent pattern: gain initial access (often via phishing or zero-day exploit), establish persistence using backdoors, escalate privileges and move laterally, then extract or sabotage data while remaining undetected for months.
APTs threaten all three pillars of the [[id:326ef9ea-d7ec-4034-967f-3465f91e8aa4][CIA Triad]] simultaneously and in a sustained manner.
Key defense tactics: Zero-trust architecture, behavior-based anomaly detection, threat hunting, MFA, and network segmentation.
The [[id:040043af-54dd-4bf3-8531-d773308c9f3a][SolarWinds Supply Chain Attack (2020)]] is the canonical modern APT example, demonstrating how nation-state actors can compromise [[id:a4c39818-dd36-4f8a-ae85-a0e8c294ac09][distributed systems]] at a scale that affects thousands of downstream organizations.

* Examples
** Threat Category Comparison
*** Extenal Threats
Origin: Outside
Intent: Malicious
Common Defenses: Firewalls, IDS/IPS, VPN

*** Internal Threats
Origin: Inside
Intent: Malicious/Accidental
Common Defenses: RBAC, logging, training

*** Environmental
Origin: Non-human/Nature
Intent: Unintentional
Common Defenses: Backups, DRP, failover systems

*** APTs
Origin: Advanced Actors
Intent: Strategic, Persistent
Common Defenses: Threat intelligence, segmentation, EDR
