#+title: IDS & IPS
#+filetags: :lecture:
* Lecture Notes
Intrusion Detection Systems (IDS) and Intrusion Prevention Systems (IPS) are two essential and complementary technologies used to identify and respond to malicious activity on networks or systems.
The key distinction is their response mode: an IDS is *passive* - it detects and alerts, but does not act. An IPS is *active* - it detects and immediately blocks or mitigates the threat.

_Analogy:_ IDS is a security guard who raises an alarm; IPS is a guard who raises an alarm *and* physically stops the attacker.

Both IDS and IPS sit within the *detective* and *preventive* layers of [[id:2f03d2a4-1d76-4da6-a7a1-492c3090953f][Defense-in-Depth]] respectively: IDS enables detection, IPS enables prevention in real time.
They directly support *Availability* and *Confidentiality* in the [[id:326ef9ea-d7ec-4034-967f-3465f91e8aa4][CIA Triad]] by identifying and stopping threats before they cause damage.

* Key Concepts
** IDS — Intrusion Detection System
An IDS monitors network or host activity and alerts administrators when potentially malicious activity is detected.
It is passive - it does not block traffic.
Deployment: Typically placed *out-of-band*, meaning it monitors a copy of traffic rather than sitting directly in the traffic path.
Limitation: Relies on security teams to take manual action after receiving alerts - response speed depends on human availability.
Use cases: Monitoring for brute-force login attempts, detecting unauthorized port scanning, and identifying lateral movement post-compromise.

** IPS — Intrusion Prevention System
An IPS monitors and *actively takes action* - it blocks or quarantines malicious traffic in real time by dropping packets, resetting connections, or quarantining hosts.
Deployment: Placed *in-line*, meaning all traffic passes through the IPS for inspection before reaching internal systems.
Often integrated with [[id:41771f87-ae23-43c3-a3fe-548cb4d38315][Firewalls]] (especially NGFWs) as a combined defense layer.
Use cases: Blocking known malware signatures before they reach internal systems, automatically stopping DDoS traffic.
Risk: Because the IPS sits in-line, false positives can block legitimate traffic - tuning is critical.

** Detection Techniques
Both IDS and IPS use one of two detection approaches:
*Signature-Based Detection* - matches traffic against a database of known attack patterns, similar to how traditional antivirus works. Fast and accurate against known threats, but completely ineffective against zero-day attacks and novel malware.
Example: blocking a packet that matches the WannaCry ransomware signature.

*Anomaly-Based Detection* - establishes a baseline of normal activity and flags anything that deviates significantly. Effective at identifying [[id:e92b2fbc-03d6-4cea-a544-b9eb0f693774][Common Cyber Threat Types]] like zero-days, insider threats, and new attack patterns. Requires tuning to avoid false positives. Example: alerting when a user downloads 100GB of data at 3AM.

** IDS vs IPS Comparison
*** Features
**** Role
IDS: Detection and alerting
IPS: Detection and active prevention

**** Response
IDS: Passive (alerts only)
IPS: Active (blocks or mitigates traffic)

**** Deployment
IDS: Out-of-band
IPS: In-line (traffic passes through)

**** Risk of False Positive
IDS: Low impact (no blocking)
IPS: High (can block legitimate traffic)

**** Speed Requirement
IDS: Lower
IPS: Higher (must act in real time)

** Best Practices
Use IDS for monitoring-only zones such as internal segments where blocking would be disruptive.
Use IPS at the perimeter to proactively stop attacks before they reach endpoints.
Regularly update signatures and tune anomaly thresholds to balance detection accuracy and false positive rates.
