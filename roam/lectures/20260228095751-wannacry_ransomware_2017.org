#+title: WannaCry Ransomware (2017)
#+filetags: :lecture:
* Lecture Notes
WannaCry is the defining case study of Lecture B, illustrating how a single unpatched vulnerability in a [[id:a4c39818-dd36-4f8a-ae85-a0e8c294ac09][distributed system]] can cascade into a global catastrophe affecting hundreds of thousands of machines across 150 countries.

It serves as a direct parallel to the [[id:e0707681-afc9-45c5-b207-1871161d0d2d][Equifax Data Breach (2017)]], which occurred the same year and shared the same root cause: a critical patch was available but never applied.

Both cases reinforce what the course calls its central patch management principle: "A patch not applied is a vulnerability waiting to be exploited."
WannaCry is also the primary real-world example for [[id:e92b2fbc-03d6-4cea-a544-b9eb0f693774][Common Cyber Threat Types]] - specifically demonstrating how ransomware (a malware subtype) and worm propagation combine to maximize damage.

* Key Concepts
** What Happened
WannaCry was a global ransomware attack that emerged on May 12, 2017.
It used a *self-propagating worm* mechanism to spread rapidly across networks - meaning it required no user interaction once it was inside a vulnerable network.
The malware encrypted files on infected systems and demanded a Bitcoin ransom for decryption.
Even air-gapped machines with no direct internet access were infected, as the worm spread laterally through internal networks.

** Spread Mechanism
WannaCry exploited *CVE-2017-0144*, a critical vulnerability in the SMBv1 (Server Message Block) protocol in Microsoft Windows.
This exploit was derived from *EternalBlue*, a cyberweapon developed by the NSA that was leaked by the Shadow Brokers hacking group.
Microsoft had released patch *MS17-010* two months earlier in March 2017 - but vast numbers of systems, especially in critical sectors and developing countries, remained unpatched.
This is the defining example of the *unpatched software* vulnerability type from [[id:a4c39818-dd36-4f8a-ae85-a0e8c294ac09][Distributed Systems]].

** Impact
- *Scale*: Over 230,000 computers infected across 150+ countries within days.
- *Healthcare*: The UK's National Health Service (NHS) was severely disrupted - hospital systems shut down, appointments cancelled, ambulances rerouted.
- *Telecommunications*: Telefónica (Spain), Megafon (Russia).
- *Transportation*: Deutsche Bahn (Germany), FedEx.
- *Financial damages*: Estimated global losses exceeded $4 billion.

** CIA Triad Violations
- *Availability*: Systems were locked and rendered inoperable — the primary attack on the CIA Triad.
- *Confidentiality*: Data was encrypted and effectively held hostage; some variants also exfiltrated data.
- *Integrity*: File system integrity was destroyed by unauthorized encryption.

* Key Lessons
1. *Patch early, patch often* - known vulnerabilities are among the most common and preventable attack vectors.
2. Organizations must *inventory all software and systems*, especially legacy infrastructure, and maintain proper update cycles.
3. *Network segmentation and least privilege access* limit lateral spread - a worm that cannot move between segments cannot scale.
4. *Backups and incident response plans* are critical: organizations with clean backups could recover without paying ransom.
5. The EternalBlue \(\rightarrow\) WannaCry chain illustrates how government-developed cyberweapons, once leaked, become devastating tools for [[id:e92b2fbc-03d6-4cea-a544-b9eb0f693774][Common Cyber Threat Types]] in the hands of criminal actors.
