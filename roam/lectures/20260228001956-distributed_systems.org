#+title: Distributed Systems
#+filetags: :lecture:
* Lecture Notes
A distributed system is a network of independent computers that work together to appear as a single coherent system to the user.
These systems share resources, process data, and communicate over a network - and because they do so across many machines over potentially insecure channels, they present a dramatically larger attack surface than a single centralized system.
Understanding distributed systems is central to [[id:0be6b1f3-fb8c-4cde-b580-f7028e347518][cybersecurity]] because the majority of modern infrastructure - cloud platforms, SaaS applications, IoT networks, and blockchain systems - is distributed by nature.
This expanded attack surface means that all three pillars of the [[id:326ef9ea-d7ec-4034-967f-3465f91e8aa4][CIA Triad]] are under pressure simultaneously: the lack of a central control point complicates confidentiality, the need to communicate across nodes creates opportunities for integrity violations, and the interdependence of components makes availability harder to guarantee.
Security threats in distributed systems fall into two related categories: *threats*, which describe the actions attackers take, and *vulnerabilities*, which describe the weaknesses those actions exploit.
The relationship between the two is important - a threat without a vulnerability to exploit cannot succeed, and a vulnerability without a threat actor to exploit it causes no harm.
Real-world breaches like the [[id:e0707681-afc9-45c5-b207-1871161d0d2d][Equifax Data Breach (2017)]] and ... illustrate concretely how threats and vulnerabilities combine to produce catastrophic outcomes in distributed environments.

* Key Concepts
** Definition and Characteristics
A *distributed system* is a network of independent computers that collectively appear as a single coherent system to end users.
Key characteristics that distinguish distributed systems from centralized ones are: no single central point of control, components operating concurrently across multiple machines, and the requirement to coordinate and communicate over potentially insecure channels.
** Common Security Threats
*Unauthorized Access* occurs when attackers gain access to sensitive resources or system components without proper credentials - typically caused by weak authentication, exposed APIs, or default passwords - and threatens all three pillars of the [[id:326ef9ea-d7ec-4034-967f-3465f91e8aa4][CIA Triad]].
*Eavesdropping* involves intercepting unencrypted data being transmitted across the network - a direct violation of *Confidentiality* - most commonly caused by the use of unencrypted protocols such as plain HTTP rather than HTTPS.
*Tampering* involves modifying data during transmission between distributed nodes - a direct violation of *Integrity* - caused by insecure communication protocols or poor data validation.
*Man-in-the-Middle (MITM) Attacks* occur when an attacker secretly intercepts and potentially alters communication between two legitimate parties, simultaneously violating both *Confidentiality* and *Integrity*. Common causes include insecure protocols, DNS spoofing, and certificate forgery.
** Vulnerabilities
A *vulnerability* is a weakness, flaw, or misconfiguration in a system that can be exploited by a [[id:][Threat Actor]] to gain unauthorized access, disrupt services, or compromise data.
*Unpatched Software* - failing to apply available security patches - allows attackers to exploit publicly known vulnerabilities. The WannaCry ransomware attack (2017) exploited an unpatched Windows vulnerability called EternalBlue.
*Default Credentials* - using factory-set usernames and passwords such as admin/admin - effectively leave systems as open doors. The Mirai botnet compromised thousands of IoT devices precisely this way.
*Exposed APIs* - publicly accessible interfaces that lack proper authentication or access control - allow attackers to extract data, manipulate system functions, or automate attacks at scale. T-Mobile's 2021 data breach stemmed from an insecure exposed API.
*Misconfigured Cloud Services* - storage or databases inadvertently made public due to poor understanding of access policies - can expose confidential data to anyone with the link, violating [[id:2df80c85-7f5a-4829-bf7c-a5bc82be2a4c][privacy]] at scale. Numerous organizations have leaked sensitive PII through misconfigured AWS S3 buckets.

* Examples
** Target Data Breach (2013)
Attackers breached Target's network not by attacking Target directly, but through a third-party HVAC vendor whose credentials were stolen - a supply chain vulnerability.
Once inside, attackers moved *laterally* through the network to reach Target's payment systems, ultimately stealing credit card data from over 40 million customers.
The key lesson is that distributed systems are only as secure as their weakest link - indirect vulnerabilities through vendors and partners can be just as devastating as direct attacks.
** Threat and Vulnerability Interaction
Eavesdropping as a threat only succeeds because unencrypted protocols exist as a vulnerability.
MITM attacks only succeed because insecure certificate validation and DNS configurations create exploitable weaknesses.
This is why defense in distributed systems must be *layered* - covering endpoints, APIs, network channels, and cloud configurations simultaneously.
