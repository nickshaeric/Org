#+title: Patch Management
#+filetags: :lecture:
* Lecture Notes
Patch management is the process of applying updates to software, operating systems, and firmware to fix security vulnerabilities, bugs, and performance issues.

It is the single most direct defense against the *unpatched software* vulnerability class identified in [[id:a4c39818-dd36-4f8a-ae85-a0e8c294ac09][Distributed Systems]], and its failure is the shared root cause of both the [[id:e0707681-afc9-45c5-b207-1871161d0d2d][Equifax Data Breach (2017)]] and the [[id:00bead97-065c-4a3b-9b43-d98a4e5cb1c2][WannaCry Ransomware (2017)]] attack.
The core principle is simple but frequently violated in practice: "A known vulnerability with no patch applied is an open door to attackers."

Patch management is broader than simply applying updates - *vulnerability management* encompasses configuration hardening, disabling unused services, and network segmentation as compensating controls when patches cannot immediately be applied.
Within the [[id:2f03d2a4-1d76-4da6-a7a1-492c3090953f][Defense-in-Depth]] framework, patching is a *preventive control* - it closes known vulnerabilities before threat actors can exploit them.

* Key Concepts
** The Patch Management Lifecycle
A structured, repeatable lifecycle is essential for effective patching at scale:
1. *Inventory* — Discover and document all assets (hardware, software, OS, applications) and identify which systems are in scope.
2. *Prioritize* — Classify patches by severity and exploitability using scores such as CVSS. Focus first on critical systems and internet-facing services.
3. *Test* — Apply patches in a controlled test environment first to check for compatibility, functionality regressions, and unintended disruptions.
4. *Deploy* — Roll out patches using automated tools across production systems. Stagger rollouts for critical systems to maintain availability.
5. *Verify* — Confirm patches were successfully applied and vulnerabilities are no longer exploitable. Generate compliance reports.

** Challenges in Patch Management
*Legacy Systems* — older systems that no longer receive vendor support cannot be patched in the traditional sense; they may require virtual patching, network isolation, or replacement.
*Downtime and Business Impact* — patching often requires reboots or temporary outages, leading organizations to delay updates and inadvertently increasing their attack surface.
*Lack of Automation* — manual patching is time-consuming, error-prone, and not scalable across large distributed environments.
*Asset Visibility* — incomplete inventories mean unknown or forgotten devices remain unpatched and exposed — a direct failure of the Inventory phase.

* Examples
** WannaCry and Equifax: The Cost of Not Patching
The [[id:00bead97-065c-4a3b-9b43-d98a4e5cb1c2][WannaCry Ransomware (2017)]] exploited EternalBlue (CVE-2017-0144), for which Microsoft had released patch MS17-010 two months before the attack.
The [[id:e0707681-afc9-45c5-b207-1871161d0d2d][Equifax Data Breach (2017)]] exploited Apache Struts (CVE-2017-5638), for which a patch had been available for months before the breach.
Both cases share an identical failure in the Lifecycle: the Inventory and Deploy phases were not executed - systems were not tracked, and patches were not applied.
Key takeaway: "Patching is not just IT hygiene - it's a critical part of your cybersecurity defense strategy."
