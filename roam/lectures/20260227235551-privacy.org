#+title: Privacy
#+filetags: :lecture:
* Lecture Notes
Privacy is the fundamental right of individuals to control the collection, use, storage, and dissemination of their personal information.

It is important to understand that privacy is not simply about secrecy - it is about *agency* and *informed consent*.
This distinction matters because a system can be technically secure while still violating privacy.

For example, a company may protect your data from external attackers while simultaneously sharing it with third parties without your knowledge - the data is secure, but your privacy has been violated.

This is why [[id:ccf64ce0-6296-46c7-92c4-3135ff15231b][privacy vs security]]  are treated as related but distinct concepts.
Privacy concerns arise across four distinct categories, each capturing a different dimension of personal space that individuals have a right to protect.

* Key Concepts
** Definition
*Privacy* is the right of individuals to control what personal information is collected about them, who can access it, under what circumstances, and for what purpose.
** Categories of Privacy
*Informational Privacy* concerns the handling of personally identifiable information (PII) - such as names, email addresses, browsing habits, health records, and financial data.

The risk is that if this data is not properly secured or governed, it can be leaked or sold to third parties without the individual's consent.
*Bodily Privacy* protects individuals from physical or biological intrusions, particularly in medical or surveillance contexts - for example, mandatory biometric scanning or medical testing without consent.
The risk here is loss of autonomy over one's own biological data, such as DNA profiles or fingerprints.

*Territorial Privacy* protects personal physical and digital spaces - including homes, private offices, personal cloud storage, and virtual desktops.
The risk is that unauthorized surveillance of these spaces, even passively, can cause real harm and erode the sense of personal safety.

*Communication Privacy* ensures that conversations and correspondence remain confidential and free from unauthorized interception.
Without encryption, emails, messages, and calls are vulnerable to interception by malicious actors or even governments.

* Examples
** Real-World Privacy Breaches
The *Facebook–Cambridge Analytica scandal* is a case of informational privacy violation at scale - a third-party app harvested personal data from millions of users without explicit consent, which was then used to influence political behaviour.
*Mobile app permissions* represent a subtler but pervasive form of privacy erosion - many apps request access to location, contacts, camera, and microphone far beyond what their core functionality requires, and users frequently grant these permissions without fully understanding the implications.
*Smart devices* such as fitness trackers, smart TVs, and voice assistants continuously gather behavioural data. When this data is transmitted to external servers without proper encryption or consent, it constitutes a serious territorial and informational privacy concern.
*Email scanning by service providers* - older versions of Gmail, for instance - used to scan message content to target advertising, undermining communication privacy even within a system users trusted for correspondence.
