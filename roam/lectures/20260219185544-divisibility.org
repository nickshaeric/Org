#+title: Divisibility
#+filetags: :lecture:
* Lecture Notes
** Definition
- Divisibility: $a,b \in \mathbb{Z}$
  $a \mid b \iff a \ne 0$ and $b=ak$ for some integer $k$.
  $a \mid b$ = "$b$ is divisible by $a$"
        "$a$ is a divisor of $b$"
        "$b$ is a mulitple of $a$"

* Key Concepts
- Basic Properties of Divisibility
  - If $a \mid b$ and $a \mid c$, then $a \mid (mb + nc)$ for any $m,n \in \mathbb{Z}$.
  - If $a \mid b$, then $a \mid bc$ for any $c \in \mathbb{Z}$.
  - If $a \mid b$ and $b \mid c$, then $a \mid c$.

- Reflexive & Unit Facts
  - $a \mid a$ for all $a \ne 0$.
  - $1 \mid a$ for all integers $a$.
  - $a \mid 0$ for all $a \ne 0$.

- Sign Rules
  - If $a \mid b$, then $a \mid (-b)$ and $(-a) \mid b$.
  - Divisibility ignores sign: divisors can be positive or negative.

- Prime Connection
  - If $p$ is prime and $p \mid bc$, then $p \mid b$ or $p \mid c$.

- Divisibility as an Algebraic Condition
  - Showing $a \mid b$ means: find an integer $k$ such that $b = ak$.

- Proof Strategy Reminder
  - To prove divisibility: start from the definition and construct the integer.
  - To disprove a divisibility statement: find a counterexample.

* Examples
** Example 1
$\forall a,b,c \in \mathbb{Z}$, if $a \mid b$ and $b \mid c$, then $a \mid bc$.
Answer: True

Proof: Suppose $a,b,c \in \mathbb{Z}$.
Suppose $a \mid b$, that is $a \ne 0$ and $\exists x \in \mathbb{Z}$ such that $b=ax$.
Suppose $b \mid c$, that is $b \neq 0$ and $\exists y \in \mathbb{Z}$ such that $c = by$.
$c = by = axy$, let $k = xy$, $k \in \mathbb{Z}$ therefore $c = ak$. Since $a$
is a factor of $c$, $a \mid c$.

** Example 2
$\forall a,b,c \in \mathbb{Z}$, if $a \mid bc$, then $a \mid b$ or $a \mid c$.
Answer: False
Negation: $\exists a,b,c \in \mathbb{Z}$ such that $a \mid bc$, but $a \nmid b$ and $a \nmid c$.

Proof: Let $a=6$, $b=2$, and $c=3$, where $a,b,c \in \mathbb{Z}$ and $a \ne 0$.
$a \mid bc = 6 \mid (2)(3) = 6 \mid 6 \implies \exists x \in \mathbb{Z}$ such that $6=6x$,
let $x=1$, where $1 \in \mathbb{Z}$, therefore $6=6(1)$.
But, $6 \nmid 2$, since there does not exists an integer $y$ such that $2=6y$ and $6 \nmid 3$ since there does not exists an integer $z$ such that $3=6z$, therefore the original statement is false by negation.

** Example 3
$\forall a, b, c \in \mathbb{Z}$, if $a \mid b$ and $a \mid c$, then $a \mid b + c$ and $a \mid b - c$.
Answer: True

Proof: Suppose $a,b,c \in \mathbb{Z}$. Suppose $a \mid b$ and $a \mid c$, that is $a \ne 0$ and $\exists x,y \in \mathbb{Z}$ such that $b=ax$ and $c=ay$.
Then $b+c=ax+ay=a(x+y)$, since $a$ is a factor of $b+c$, $a \mid b+c$.
Likewise, $b-c=ax-ay=a(x-y)$, since $a$ is a factor $b-c$, $a \mid b-c$.
