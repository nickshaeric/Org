#+title: Inductive Arguments
#+filetags: :lecture:
* Lecture Notes
Not all good arguments are deductively [[id:d396a0fe-e712-41ce-86c2-7916e7f2ec7a][valid]].
*Inductive arguments* generalize from observations about past or particular cases to a conclusion about a future or general case - but the conclusion goes *beyond* what the premises strictly guarantee.

This means that even a good inductive argument is not deductively valid: it is always possible to construct a counterexample, since the premises do not *entail* the conclusion - they merely make it more probable or plausible.

This course is specifically concerned with *deductive* [[id:d396a0fe-e712-41ce-86c2-7916e7f2ec7a][validity]] and [[id:492af8e6-8800-437a-8051-a38168733496][consequence & entailment]].
Inductive arguments fall outside the scope of formal logic as studied here.

* Key Concepts
** Definition: Inductive Argument
An *inductive argument* is an argument in which the premises support the conclusion by generalizing from observed or past cases, without the premises *entailing* the conclusion.

A good inductive argument makes the conclusion *probable* given the premises - but it is never deductively valid.

** Connection to Proof by Induction
Inductive arguments bear a conceptual resemblance to *proof by induction* in mathematics.
In both cases, observations or base cases are used to support a general conclusion.
However, mathematical induction is actually a *deductively valid* proof technique - it establishes its conclusion with certainty given the base case and inductive step.
Natural language inductive arguments do not have this guarantee.
The shared name reflects the shared intuition of "generalizing from cases" but the logical status of the two is quite different.

** Notes
- Do not say the premises of an inductive argument *infer* the conclusion - entailment is a relation between sentences, inference is something an agent does.
- Inductive arguments can be *good* arguments even though they are not deductively valid.
  Validity is not the only standard by which arguments can be evaluated.

* Examples
** Example 1 (Good but Invalid Inductive Argument)
1. Every winter so far, it has snowed in Calgary.
\(\therefore\) It will snow in Calgary this coming winter.
Counterexample: this coming winter it does not snow in Calgary.
The premise can be true and the conclusion false - *not deductively valid*.
Yet this is a reasonable argument in everyday reasoning - it is a good *inductive* argument.
