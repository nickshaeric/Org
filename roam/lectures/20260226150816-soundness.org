#+title: Soundness
#+filetags: :lecture:
* Lecture Notes
*Soundness* is a stronger property than [[id:d396a0fe-e712-41ce-86c2-7916e7f2ec7a][validity]].
A valid argument guarantees that *if* the premises are true, the conclusion must be true.
But validity alone does not tell us whether the premises actually are true.

Soundness closes this gap - a sound argument is one where the structure is valid *and* the premises are actually true. This combination guarantees that the conclusion is true.

Importantly, the logician is primarily concerned with validity rather than soundness.
Determining whether premises are actually true falls outside formal logic - that is the job of science, history, observation, and so on. Logic concerns itself with the *conditions* under which statements are true, not with *which* statements are true.

* Key Concepts
** Definition: Soundness
An argument is *sound* \(\iff :\)
1. It is [[id:d396a0fe-e712-41ce-86c2-7916e7f2ec7a][valid]], *and*
2. All of its premises are *true*.

** Rebutting an Argument
To rebut an argument you have exactly two options:
- Show that *one or more premises are false* - attacking soundness.
- Show that *the argument is not valid* - attacking the structure.

** Notes
- Soundness implies validity, but validity does not imply soundness.
- A sound argument *guarantees* a true conclusion.
- A valid argument with false premises is *not* sound — see Example 1.
- Formal logic focuses on validity rather than soundness since establishing
  the actual truth of premises is an empirical matter, not a logical one.

* Examples
** Example 1 (Valid but Unsound)
1. Oranges are either fruit or musical instruments.
2. Oranges are not fruit.
\(\therefore\) Oranges are musical instruments.
The argument is *valid* but *unsound* - premise 2 is false.

** Example 2 (Valid but Unsound)
1. Abe Lincoln was either from France or from Luxembourg.
2. Abe Lincoln was not from Luxembourg.
\(\therefore\) Abe Lincoln was from France.
*Valid* structure, but premise 1 is false - Lincoln was from the United States. *Unsound.*

** Example 3 (Sound)
1. All humans are mortal.
2. Socrates is a human.
\(\therefore\) Socrates is mortal.
The argument is *valid* and both premises are *true*. *Sound.*
The conclusion is therefore guaranteed to be true.
