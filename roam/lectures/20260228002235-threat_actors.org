#+title: Threat Actors
#+filetags: :lecture:
* Lecture Notes
In [[id:0be6b1f3-fb8c-4cde-b580-f7028e347518][Cybersecurity]], understanding *who* the attackers are is just as important as understanding *how* they attack.
Threat actors are individuals or groups who pose a risk to digital assets, each characterized by distinct motivations, capabilities, and targets.
The type of threat actor determines not just the method of attack but also its likely target, sophistication, and persistence - and therefore shapes what defenses are most appropriate.

It is also worth noting that the same vulnerability in a [[id:a4c39818-dd36-4f8a-ae85-a0e8c294ac09][distributed system]] can be exploited by very different actors for very different purposes - an unpatched API might be hit opportunistically by a script kiddie, systematically by a cybercriminal, or as part of a years-long campaign by a nation-state actor.

A key insight from the lecture is that threat sophistication does not correlate cleanly with threat severity - even low-skill actors can cause catastrophic damage if security hygiene is poor, and many major breaches begin with something as simple as a phishing email.
This matters for the [[id:326ef9ea-d7ec-4034-967f-3465f91e8aa4][CIA Triad]] because different actors tend to target different pillars: hacktivists often target *Availability* through DDoS attacks, cybercriminals frequently target *Confidentiality* through data theft, and nation-state actors may target all three simultaneously in sustained operations.

* Key Concepts
** Hacktivists
*Hacktivists* are motivated by ideological or political causes - protest, activism, or the public exposure of corruption.
Their typical tactics include DDoS attacks, website defacement, and leaking sensitive information.
The group Anonymous is the canonical example, having targeted governments, corporations, and religious institutions in various protest campaigns.
An important nuance: hacktivists often frame their actions as ethical or justified, but they still cause real harm to systems, data integrity, and innocent third parties - the motivation does not neutralize the damage.
** Insiders
*Insiders* are threat actors who already have legitimate access to internal systems - employees, contractors, vendors, or partners.
Their motivation varies widely: it may be malicious intent, revenge, financial gain, or simple negligence.
Malicious insiders may steal trade secrets or sabotage systems deliberately; negligent insiders may click phishing links or misconfigure access controls without any harmful intent.
Insider threats are among the hardest to detect precisely because the actor is already trusted and bypasses traditional perimeter defenses entirely - the threat originates from within the secured boundary rather than outside it.
This connects directly to the [[id:2df80c85-7f5a-4829-bf7c-a5bc82be2a4c][privacy]] concern of over-collection: the more data an organization collects and the more people have access to it, the larger the insider threat surface becomes.
** Cybercriminals
*Cybercriminals* are motivated primarily by financial gain and represent the most common category of attacker encountered in practice.
They operate in organized crime rings, frequently selling stolen data and attack tools on dark web markets and hacker forums.
Their tactics include ransomware, phishing, identity theft, online fraud, and credit card skimming - all of which map directly onto violations of [[id:326ef9ea-d7ec-4034-967f-3465f91e8aa4][CIA Triad]] pillars, particularly *Confidentiality* and *Availability*.
A critical insight is that cybercriminals treat attacks as a *business* - complete with affiliate networks, profit-sharing schemes, and even customer support portals for ransomware victims. The Conti ransomware group is a prominent example, having extorted millions from organizations globally.
** Nation-State Actors
*Nation-state actors* are government-sponsored groups motivated by espionage, geopolitical advantage, critical infrastructure disruption, or economic sabotage.
They are highly trained, well-funded, and capable of executing stealthy long-term operations known as *Advanced Persistent Threats (APTs)* - attacks that may remain undetected for months or years.
Their targets tend to be government agencies, defense contractors, critical utilities, and strategic corporations.
The Stuxnet worm - believed to be a joint U.S.-Israeli cyberweapon targeting Iran's nuclear centrifuge program - is the defining example of nation-state offensive cyber capability.
The [[id:][SolarWinds Supply Chain Attack (2020)]], attributed to APT29 (Cozy Bear), a Russian intelligence group, illustrates how nation-state actors can compromise [[id:a4c39818-dd36-4f8a-ae85-a0e8c294ac09][distributed systems]] at a scale that affects thousands of downstream organizations simultaneously.
** Script Kiddies
*Script kiddies* are inexperienced individuals who use pre-built hacking tools and scripts without understanding how they work, motivated by reputation, curiosity, or thrill-seeking.
Their typical tactics include port scanning, website defacement, and exploiting known unpatched vulnerabilities.
Despite their lack of technical sophistication, script kiddies can still cause significant damage - particularly to systems with poor security hygiene - because the tools they use can be highly effective even in unskilled hands.
This reinforces the core lesson: many major breaches begin not with sophisticated zero-day exploits but with low-tech attacks like phishing and social engineering, often executed by unskilled actors exploiting basic vulnerabilities.

* Examples
** Threat Actor and Attack Type Mapping
Hacktivists tend to target *Availability* - DDoS attacks against websites and services are their most visible weapon.
Cybercriminals tend to target *Confidentiality* - stealing and monetizing data is their primary goal - as illustrated by the [[id:e0707681-afc9-45c5-b207-1871161d0d2d][Equifax Data Breach (2017)]].
Nation-state actors tend to target all three pillars simultaneously in sustained campaigns - as illustrated by the [[id:][SolarWinds Supply Chain Attack (2020)]].
Insiders can violate any pillar depending on their access level and intent, making them uniquely dangerous and uniquely difficult to defend against.
Script kiddies most commonly target *Availability* and exploit *Confidentiality* weaknesses through opportunistic use of known tools against unpatched systems.
