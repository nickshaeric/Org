#+title: Euclidean Algorithm
#+filetags: :lecture:
* Lecture Notes
Euclidean Algorithm = An algorithm used to compute \(\gcd{(a,b)}\).

\(\gcd{(a,b)}\) = The [[id:010125cd-5767-44d0-9dcf-bd3453bf8cde][Greatest Common Divisor]] of \(a\) and \(b\).

- \(\gcd{(a,0)} = a\), when \(a>0\).
- The algorithm repeatedly applies the division algorithm:
  \(a=bq+r\), where \(0 \le r < \left| b  \right|\).

** Computing a [[id:be0dc7a5-4436-4403-867e-daa060b8c469][Integral Linear Combination]]
The table method is a structured extension of the Euclidean Algorithm that computes coefficients \(x,y\) such that \(\gcd(a,b)=ax+by\).
[[id:c3576183-80f4-4db4-9182-13ce1788a337][Table Method for Computing Integral Linear Combinations]]

* Key Concepts
** Definition
Let \(a\) and \(b\) be integers such that not both are zero.
The Euclidean Algorithm computes \(\gcd{(a,b)}\) by repeatedly replacing \((a,b)\) with \((b,r)\) where \(r\) is the remainder when \(a\) si divided by \(b\).

The process terminates when the remainder becomes \(0\).
The last nonzero remainder is the greatest common divisor.

** Notes
- Each step strictly decreases the remainder.
- The algorithm always terminates after finitely many steps.
- The method works for any integer (not both zero).

* Examples
** Example 1
\(\gcd(98,72)\)

\begin{align*}
98 &= 1(72)+26\\
72 &= 2(26)+20\\
26 &= 1(20)+6\\
20 &= 3(6)+2\\
6  &= 3(2)+0
\end{align*}

\[\gcd{(98,72)}=2\]
