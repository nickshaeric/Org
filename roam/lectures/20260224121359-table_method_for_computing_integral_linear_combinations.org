#+title: Table Method for Computing Integral Linear Combinations
#+filetags: :lecture:
* Definition
The table method is a systematic procedure used to compute integers \(x\) and \(y\) such that

\[
\gcd(a,b) = ax + by
\]

for integers \(a\) and \(b\) not both zero. It is a structured form of the extended Euclidean algorithm.

* Foundational Lemma
If \(a = qb + r\), then

\[
\gcd(a,b) = \gcd(b,r).
\]

This lemma allows repeated replacement of a pair by a smaller pair until a remainder of \(0\) is obtained.

* Algorithm
Given integers \(a\) and \(b\):

1. Apply the Euclidean algorithm to compute successive remainders.
2. Record each remainder.
3. Construct a table expressing each remainder as a linear combination of \(a\) and \(b\).
4. The last nonzero remainder is \(\gcd(a,b)\).
5. The corresponding row provides integers \(x,y\) such that
   \[
   ax + by = \gcd(a,b).
   \]

* Example
Find integers \(x\) and \(y\) such that
\(\gcd(2026,271) = 2026x + 271y\).

** Step 1 — Euclidean Algorithm
\begin{align*}
2026 &= 7(271) + 129\\
271  &= 2(129) + 13\\
129  &= 9(13) + 12\\
13   &= 1(12) + 1\\
12   &= 12(1) + 0
\end{align*}

\[
\gcd(2026,271) = 1
\]

** Step 2 — Table Construction
Each value is written as a linear combination of \(2026\) and \(271\).

\begin{array}{c|cc}
\text{Value} & 2026 & 271 \\\hline
2026 & 1 & 0 \\
271  & 0 & 1 \\
129  & 1 & -7 \\
13   & -2 & 15 \\
12   & 19 & -142 \\
1    & -21 & 157
\end{array}

** Step 3 — Result

\[
1 = -21(2026) + 157(271)
\]

Thus,
\[
x = -21,\quad y = 157.
\]

* Why the Method Works
Each remainder is computed from previous remainders. Since earlier values are already written as linear combinations of \(a\) and \(b\), substitution ensures every new remainder is also a linear combination of \(a\) and \(b\). Therefore the final nonzero remainder, which equals \(\gcd(a,b)\), must also have this form.

* Properties
- The algorithm always terminates after finitely many steps.
- A solution \(x,y\) always exists.
- If \(\gcd(a,b)=1\), then \(1\) can be written as a linear combination of \(a\) and \(b\).
- The coefficients are not unique.
