#+title: Privacy vs Security
#+filetags: :lecture:
* Lecture Notes
[[id:0be6b1f3-fb8c-4cde-b580-f7028e347518][Cybersecurity]] & [[id:2df80c85-7f5a-4829-bf7c-a5bc82be2a4c][Privacy]] are closely related but address fundamentally different concerns, and conflating them is a common source of confusion.

Security is primarily a technical and operational discipline - it asks "how do we defend systems and data from unauthorized access or attack?"

Privacy is primarily a rights-based and ethical discipline - it asks "who should be permitted to access this data, under what conditions, and with whose consent?"

The key insight is that security is a *tool* that can serve privacy, but security alone does not guarantee privacy.
A well-secured system can still violate privacy if the data it protects is being collected or used in ways the individual has not consented to.

Conversely, privacy policies and frameworks depend on security controls to actually be enforceable - without encryption and access control, privacy guarantees are meaningless in practice.
The two concepts are therefore best understood as *complementary and mutually dependent*, each operating in a different register.

* Key Concepts
** Focus
*Privacy* is concerned with protecting personal rights - autonomy, dignity, and individual control over personal data.
*Security* is concerned with defending systems, data, and networks from unauthorized access, attacks, and damage.
** What Drives Each
Privacy is rooted in laws, ethics, and human rights frameworks - such as GDPR, HIPAA, and PIPEDA - and is fundamentally about fairness and individual choice in how data is handled.
Security is driven by technical standards, risk management, and operational protocols designed to reduce vulnerabilities and exposure to threats.
** Who Manages Each
Privacy is overseen by individuals, organizations, and legal or regulatory bodies - including data protection officers, compliance officers, and policymakers.
Security is handled by IT professionals such as security engineers, system administrators, and cybersecurity teams who implement controls and monitor for threats.
** Goal
The goal of privacy is to ensure informed consent, transparency, and trust in how personal data is collected, shared, and used.
The goal of security is to prevent breaches, unauthorized access, data leaks, and other malicious actions that compromise systems and information.

* Examples
** The Email Analogy
This distinction is illustrated cleanly through the example of email.
Security can encrypt your emails to prevent anyone from reading them during transmission - this protects the *channel*.
Privacy ensures that only the people you have authorized can read those emails, and that no unnecessary data - such as metadata or contact lists - is shared without your consent - this protects your *rights*.
A system can provide the former without the latter.
