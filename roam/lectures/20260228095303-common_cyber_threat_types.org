#+title: Common Cyber Threat Types
#+filetags: :lecture:
* Lecture Notes
While [[id:4be1a51f-d94f-4c23-8b1a-2cbdbbec45fd][Security Threat Categories]]  organizes threats by origin and intent, this node focuses on the specific *types* of attacks that threat actors execute - the technical mechanisms through which harm is delivered.

Each threat type exploits different weaknesses: some target software vulnerabilities, others target human behavior, and others exploit systemic issues like password reuse.

A core principle from the lecture applies here: "Attackers only need one way in - defenders must block them all."
Understanding threat types is foundational to selecting the right [[id:][Defense-in-Depth]] controls, as each threat type has corresponding defensive responses.
All threat types ultimately map onto violations of one or more pillars of the [[id:326ef9ea-d7ec-4034-967f-3465f91e8aa4][CIA Triad]].

* Key Concepts
** Malware
*Malware* (malicious software) is a broad category of software designed to disrupt, damage, or gain unauthorized access to computer systems.
Key subtypes:
- *Viruses*: Attach to legitimate files and replicate when run.
- *Worms*: Self-replicating programs that spread across networks without user action - WannaCry operated as a worm.
- *Trojans*: Disguised as legitimate software but perform hidden malicious tasks.
- *Ransomware*: Encrypts data and demands payment for decryption - violates both *Confidentiality* and *Availability* in the [[id:326ef9ea-d7ec-4034-967f-3465f91e8aa4][CIA Triad]].
- *Spyware*: Secretly collects keystrokes, browsing activity, or personal information — a direct violation of [[id:2df80c85-7f5a-4829-bf7c-a5bc82be2a4c][privacy]].
Delivery methods include email attachments, infected USB drives, and drive-by downloads from compromised websites.
Real-world example: [[id:][WannaCry Ransomware (2017)]] caused over $4 billion in global damage.

** Phishing
*Phishing* is a social engineering attack in which attackers impersonate legitimate entities to trick users into revealing credentials or financial information, typically via fake emails or websites.
Key variants:
- *Spear Phishing*: Targeted at specific individuals (e.g., HR officers, researchers).
- *Whaling*: Targets high-level executives (CFOs, CEOs) with tailored attacks.
- *Vishing*: Voice-based phishing via phone calls.
- *Smishing*: SMS-based phishing via text messages.
Phishing is the most common initial access vector for APTs and cybercriminals alike — the Sony Pictures hack (2014) began with a spear phishing email.
Defense: Security awareness training, email filtering, and MFA.

** Insider Threats
See [[id:41aad614-a6f6-4be2-b524-f2bc6897c85a][Threat Actors]] for full treatment. Key point: Edward Snowden's leak of classified NSA documents is the canonical malicious insider example.
Mitigations: Least privilege policies, employee monitoring, exit protocols, and audit trails.

** DoS and DDoS
*Denial of Service (DoS)* and *Distributed Denial of Service (DDoS)* attacks flood a server, service, or network with excessive traffic to render it unavailable - a direct attack on *Availability* in the [[id:326ef9ea-d7ec-4034-967f-3465f91e8aa4][CIA Triad]].
DoS originates from a single machine; DDoS coordinates the attack across many compromised machines (a botnet).
DDoS attacks are also used as a *smokescreen* - distracting IT teams while a concurrent data breach is executed, simultaneously attacking *Confidentiality*.
The 2016 Mirai botnet - which exploited default credentials on IoT devices - took down major services including Netflix and Twitter.
Defense: Rate limiting, web application firewalls (WAFs), and cloud-based DDoS protection.

** Credential Stuffing
*Credential stuffing* is the automated injection of username/password combinations obtained from past data breaches into other websites and services.
It exploits the common human behavior of password reuse - a breach at one platform (e.g., LinkedIn) enables account takeovers on banks, emails, or social platforms.
This attack type sits at the intersection of [[id:2df80c85-7f5a-4829-bf7c-a5bc82be2a4c][privacy]] and security: it is only possible because prior privacy breaches exposed credentials in the first place.
Mitigation: MFA, monitoring for unusual login behavior, password reuse policies, and breach notification tools.

** Zero-Day Exploits
A *zero-day exploit* targets a vulnerability that is unknown to the software vendor or public, with no patch available at the time of exploitation.
Because no defenses are prepared, detection is extremely difficult until damage is done.
Zero-days are predominantly used by nation-state actors and APTs - as in the [[id:041aad614-a6f6-4be2-b524-f2bc6897c85a][Threat Actors]] node, Stuxnet exploited four simultaneous zero-days.
Zero-days represent the upper limit of what [[id:][Patch Management]] cannot address proactively - they require behavior-based detection rather than signature-based detection.
Defense: Behavioral anomaly detection, Endpoint Detection and Response (EDR), and threat hunting.

* Examples
** Threat Type to CIA Triad Mapping
- Ransomware \(\rightarrow\) Confidentiality + Availability
- Phishing \(\rightarrow\) Confidentiality (credential theft)
- DDoS \(\rightarrow\) Availability (primary), Confidentiality (smokescreen)
- Spyware \(\rightarrow\) Confidentiality + Privacy
- Zero-Day \(\rightarrow\) All three pillars depending on payload
- Credential Stuffing \(\rightarrow\) Confidentiality + Privacy
