#+title: Logical Arguments
#+filetags: :lecture:
* Lecture Notes
An argument is a structured set of sentences composed of one or more *premises* and exactly one *conclusion*.
The premises are intended to support the conclusion.
Since arguments are built entirely from sentences, only [[id:0e5aee54-8155-4f8c-b151-d3acfef79a2a][statements]] - sentences that can be true or false - can serve as premises or conclusions. Questions and commands have no truth value and therefore cannot appear in a logical argument.

When evaluating an argument, the central question is:
*Does the conclusion follow from the premises?*
This is a question about the *structure* of the argument, not about whether the premises are actually true.
An argument can have a valid structure even if its premises are false - see [[id:d396a0fe-e712-41ce-86c2-7916e7f2ec7a][validity]] and [[id:202c1e49-0083-4123-8d97-cf06ef7f0821][soundness]].

* Key Concepts
** Definition
An *argument* is a set of [[id:0e5aee54-8155-4f8c-b151-d3acfef79a2a][statements]] consisting of:
- One or more *premises*: statements offered as support.
- Exactly one *conclusion*: the statement the premises are intended to establish.
** Indicator Words
Natural language arguments use certain words to signal the logical role of a sentence.
*Conclusion indicators* signal that what follows is the conclusion:
- so, therefore, hence, thus, accordingly, consequently
** Notes
- Premises and conclusions do not need to appear in any fixed order - a conclusion can come before its premises in natural language.
- The logical role of a sentence (premise vs. conclusion) is determined by its function in the argument, not its position.
- Indicator words are a useful guide but are not always present - sometimes the structure must be inferred from context.
- In formal logic, the distinction between premises and conclusion is made explicit by the turnstile notation: \(P_1, P_2 \vdash C\).

* Examples
** Example 1
"It is sunny. So I should take my sunglasses."
- Premise: "It is sunny."
- Conclusion indicator: "So"
- Conclusion: "I should take my sunglasses."
** Example 2
"It must have been sunny. I did wear my sunglasses after all."
- Premise indicator: "after all"
- Premise: "I did wear my sunglasses."
- Conclusion: "It must have been sunny."
(Note: the conclusion appears *first* here — order does not determine logical role.)
** Example 3
"Miss Scarlet and Professor Plum were in the study at the time of the murder.
Reverend Green had the candlestick in the ballroom, and we know there is no blood on his hands.
Hence Colonel Mustard did it in the kitchen with the lead pipe. Recall, after all, the gun had not been fired."
- Premises: all sentences except the conclusion.
- Conclusion indicator: "Hence"
- Conclusion: "Colonel Mustard did it in the kitchen with the lead pipe."
