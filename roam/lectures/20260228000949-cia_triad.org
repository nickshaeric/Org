#+title: CIA Triad
#+filetags: :lecture:
* Lecture Notes
The CIA Triad is the foundational model for understanding and implementing information security principles.
It stands for *Confidentiality*, *Integrity*, and *Availability* - three pillars that guide the design, assessment, and management of secure systems.
As introduced in [[id:0be6b1f3-fb8c-4cde-b580-f7028e347518][cybersecurity]], these three principles form the stated goal of the entire discipline - every major security framework and risk management strategy can be traced back to one or more of them.
It is also worth situating the triad relative to [[id:ccf64ce0-6296-46c7-92c4-3135ff15231b][privacy vs security]]: the CIA Triad is fundamentally a *security* framework - it tells us how to defend systems and data.
However, *Confidentiality* in particular operates as the bridge between security and [[id:2df80c85-7f5a-4829-bf7c-a5bc82be2a4c][privacy]] - enforcing confidentiality through encryption and access control is the primary technical mechanism through which privacy rights are made enforceable in practice.
Without confidentiality controls, privacy policies and frameworks are simply unenforceable promises.
It is worth noting that the three pillars are not independent - attacks often target multiple pillars simultaneously.
For example, a ransomware attack violates both *Availability* (by locking users out of their data) and *Confidentiality* (by potentially exfiltrating that data before encrypting it).
Similarly, a man-in-the-middle attack violates both *Confidentiality* (by intercepting data) and *Integrity* (by potentially altering it in transit).
Understanding the triad therefore means not just knowing each pillar in isolation, but recognizing how violations interact across them.

* Key Concepts
** Confidentiality
*Confidentiality* ensures that sensitive information is accessible only to those with proper authorization - the goal is to prevent data leakage or unauthorized disclosure.
This pillar is the technical counterpart to [[id:2df80c85-7f5a-4829-bf7c-a5bc82be2a4c][privacy]] - where privacy is the right, confidentiality is the mechanism through which that right is enforced.
The intuition is simple: like a locked filing cabinet, only people with the key should be able to access its contents.
The primary techniques for enforcing confidentiality are:
- *Encryption* converts data into an unreadable format that can only be decoded with the correct decryption key - common algorithms include AES and RSA.
- *Access Control* restricts access to data based on user roles and permissions - for example, distinguishing between read-only and administrator access.
- *Authentication* verifies a user's identity before granting access - mechanisms include passwords, biometrics, and two-factor authentication.
** Integrity
*Integrity* ensures that data is accurate, consistent, and unaltered except by authorized processes - it protects against tampering, whether accidental or malicious.
The intuition: like a sealed document, if the seal is broken you know someone has altered it.
The primary techniques for enforcing integrity are:
- *Hashing* generates a unique digital fingerprint of data - for example using SHA-256 - such that any alteration to the data produces a different fingerprint, immediately revealing tampering.
- *Checksums and CRCs* serve a similar purpose in file transfers, detecting corruption or unauthorized modification during transmission.
- *Version Control* maintains a full history of changes to data or code - Git is the canonical example in software development.
- *Audit Trails* log who changed what and when, providing accountability and supporting forensic analysis after an incident.
** Availability
*Availability* ensures that authorized users have reliable access to systems and data when they need it - a system that is perfectly secure but inaccessible is still a failure.
The intuition: like a 24/7 ATM, users expect access to their resources at any time without delay.
The primary techniques for enforcing availability are:
- *Redundancy* runs multiple servers or systems in parallel so that no single point of failure can bring down the whole system - examples include RAID storage and load balancers.
- *Failover Systems* automatically switch to backup infrastructure if a primary system fails.
- *Regular Backups* protect against data loss from ransomware, hardware failure, or accidental deletion.
- *DoS/DDoS Protection* uses firewalls and traffic filtering to absorb or deflect malicious traffic spikes before they overwhelm the system.

* Examples
** Confidentiality in Practice
A healthcare application encrypts patient records such that only the treating physician - not even IT staff - can view them, enforcing confidentiality through encryption and role-based access control.
This is also a privacy protection in practice - the encryption is the security mechanism, but the underlying motivation is protecting the patient's [[id:2df80c85-7f5a-4829-bf7c-a5bc82be2a4c][privacy]] rights over their medical data.
** Integrity in Practice
Banking systems attach hash values to transaction records during processing - if any value is altered in transit, the hash will no longer match, immediately flagging the tampering.
** Availability in Practice
Cloud services like Google Drive replicate data across multiple geographically distributed servers, ensuring users can always access their files even if one data centre goes offline.
** Cross-Pillar Violation
The Equifax breach (see [[id:e0707681-afc9-45c5-b207-1871161d0d2d][Equifax Data Breach (2017)]]) illustrates how a single incident can violate multiple pillars simultaneously - confidentiality was violated through data exfiltration, integrity was undermined by the undetected presence of attackers in the system, and availability was threatened during the incident response period.
