#+title: Radians & degrees
#+filetags: :topic:
* Definition
** What is a radian?
A /radian/ is a unit for measuring angles based on the radius of a circle.
It is defined as the angle at the center of a circle that subtends an arc whose length is equal to the radius of the circle.

* Key Points
** Why use radians
Radians provide a mathematically natural way to measure angles because they relate directly to the geometry of the circle and arc length.
In Calculus and higher mathematics, many formulas (e.g., derivatives of trig functions) are simplest when angles are expressed in radians.

** Relationship to circle measurements
- One complete revolution around a circle corresponds to:
  - \(360^{{\circ}}\)
  - \(2\pi\) radians in radian measure
  - Therefore \(360^{\circ} = 2\pi\) radians and \(180^{\circ}=\pi\) radians.

** Conversion formulas
- Degrees to Radians: \(\text{radians} = \text{degrees} \times \frac{\pi}{180}\)

- Radians to Degrees: \(\text{degrees} = \text{radians} \times \frac{180}{\pi}\)

** Intuitive understanding
Since radians are based on the arc length relative to the radius:
- If the arc length = radius, the angle = 1 radian.
- A full circle has circumference \(2\pi r\), which corresponds to \(2\pi\) radians when \(r\) is factored out.
